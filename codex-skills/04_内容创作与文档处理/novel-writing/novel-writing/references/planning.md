# Planning

Use this file before drafting fiction prose.

## Goal

Define what the chapter or scene must accomplish before writing sentences.

## Required Outputs

For every planning pass, produce:

- `Main task`: the primary narrative job of the chapter
- `Secondary task`: the extra job the chapter also carries
- `POV limit`: what the viewpoint can and cannot know
- `Required characters`: who must appear or be felt
- `Required information`: what the reader must understand by the end
- `Forbidden mistakes`: what this chapter must not accidentally do

## Planning Sequence

### 1. Name the chapter job

Choose the main job in plain language. Examples:

- introduce a new core character
- escalate suspicion
- recover a clue
- mislead the reader without lying
- move the protagonist from confusion to a usable conclusion

If you cannot name the job, the chapter is not planned yet.

### 2. Name the secondary job

Many chapters do two things, but one must dominate.

Examples:

- main job: show a rescue attempt failing
- secondary job: reveal that the apparent helper is also dangerous

### 3. Lock the point of view

State:

- whose perception centers the scene
- what that person can observe directly
- what they can only guess
- what must remain hidden from them

Do not write planning notes that exceed the chosen viewpoint unless the final prose will change viewpoint.

### 4. Decide information order

List the order in which the reader should learn things.

Useful pattern:

1. anchor where we are
2. anchor who matters here
3. reveal the immediate problem
4. escalate with action, discovery, or tension
5. end with a changed situation

Do not reveal everything as soon as you know it. Delayed information is valid only when the viewpoint and scene logic support the delay.

### 5. Define chapter end-state

Answer this before drafting:

- what has changed by the end
- what new pressure now exists
- what question or fear now drives the next chapter

If nothing has changed, the scene is probably dead weight.

## Common Planning Failures

- confusing "interesting material" with "chapter purpose"
- using hidden author knowledge as if the reader already has it
- overloading the chapter with too many equal priorities
- planning events but not planning what the reader understands when they happen
- leaving the ending emotionally or structurally flat

## Quick Check

Before drafting, verify all answers:

- Can I say what this chapter is for in one sentence?
- Do I know who owns the viewpoint?
- Do I know what the reader must understand by the end?
- Do I know what must remain uncertain?
- Do I know what specific change closes the chapter?
