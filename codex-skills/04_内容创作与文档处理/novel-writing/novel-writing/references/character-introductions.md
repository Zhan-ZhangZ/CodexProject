# Character Introductions

Use this file when drafting or reviewing a character's first meaningful appearance.

## Core Principle

Readers cannot track emotional meaning if they do not know who has entered the page.

Important characters must not appear as unlabelled emotion or action blobs.

## Character Entry Levels

### 1. Passing Figure

Needs only enough to avoid confusion.

Usually one or two of:

- role
- function in the moment
- one visual tag

### 2. Functional Character

A character who matters to the scene but is not yet a major axis.

Needs:

- role or identity
- relationship to the scene or another character
- one memorable trait

### 3. Core Character

A villain, ally, rival, love interest, or recurring family member.

Needs:

- who they are
- why they matter now
- why they are onstage in this scene
- how they appear to the reader at first glance
- at least one recognisable external trait
- at least one behavioural or tonal trait

## Minimum Questions for First Entry

When a core character first arrives, the prose should let the reader answer:

- Who is this person?
- Why are they here now?
- How are they tied to the current conflict?
- What is my first impression of them?
- Could I distinguish them from another character one page later?

If the answer to two or more of these is "no," the introduction is weak.

## Entry Context

A major character should not feel as if they materialize from nowhere.

When they first appear, anchor at least one of these immediately:

- who called or summoned them
- why they came here
- what role they hold in this setting
- why the viewpoint is looking at them now

If the prose says "X was there" without explaining why this presence makes sense in the current beat, the entrance is under-anchored.

## Villain First Appearance

Do not rely on later exposition to create villain presence.

A villain's first strong appearance should usually carry some combination of:

- beauty, menace, charm, or coldness in the surface image
- a mismatch between public composure and private intent
- a telling gesture
- someone else's reaction to them
- a detail that suggests capability or danger

Do not write:

- "she was dangerous" with no evidence
- pure backstory instead of present-tense presence
- emotion before identification

## Common Failure Modes

- the prose assumes the reader already knows family relations
- a major character appears before the scene explains why they are present
- a name appears before the role is clear
- a character enters through dialogue only and remains faceless
- the first impression is generic: "angry," "beautiful," "nervous"
- the prose gives role but no image, or image but no role

## Repair Tactics

If a character enters weakly, repair in this order:

1. clarify role
2. clarify relationship
3. add first-impression cue
4. add one specific visual or behavioural marker

Do not patch the problem by dumping biography unless the scene naturally allows it.

## Review Questions

- Does the prose identify the character quickly enough?
- Does the reader know why this character matters here?
- Is there a concrete image, not just an abstract label?
- If this is a villain or similarly central figure, does the entrance carry force?
