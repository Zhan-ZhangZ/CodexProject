# Realism Constraints

Use this file whenever fiction depends on real-world limits, procedures, or embodied capability.

## Core Principle

A scene may be dramatic, but it still has to obey the limits of what a person in that place, body, and role can perceive or do.

## Two Separate Checks

Always distinguish:

### Observation Rights

What can the character directly access?

- line of sight
- hearing range
- physical reach
- permitted access
- available time

### Knowledge Rights

What can the character correctly understand from that access?

- prior knowledge
- training
- age
- stress state
- incomplete information

Seeing is not understanding. Hearing is not confirmation.

Characters must not speak or think as though they know a concrete detail before they have plausibly accessed that detail.

## Institutional Realism

When using hospitals, police, schools, courts, labs, armies, or other institutions, ask:

- who is allowed where?
- what can family members actually see?
- who can give orders?
- what paperwork, protocol, or staffing constraints exist?
- what would be hidden, delayed, or filtered in ordinary practice?

Do not treat institutional spaces like open theatrical stages unless the story intentionally shows a breakdown.

## Body and Capability Limits

Check whether the body in question could plausibly:

- perceive the needed detail
- sustain the needed thought
- perform the needed action
- endure the needed timing

This is especially important for:

- infants
- the elderly
- injured people
- sedated people
- exhausted professionals

## One-Chance Contact Scenes

If a character has only one brief chance to inspect, touch, or verify something:

- write the time pressure
- write the concealment
- write the uncertainty
- do not write leisurely inspection unless the scene truly permits it

## Uncertainty Protocol

If you are not sure whether a real-world detail is correct:

- say it needs verification
- avoid writing the unsure point as settled fact
- if drafting prose anyway, use the most conservative plausible version

## Review Questions

- Could the character actually access this information?
- Could they understand what they accessed?
- Does the dialogue assume knowledge the character has not plausibly acquired yet?
- Does the institution behave plausibly enough?
- Does the body or age of the character support the action?
- Did the scene grant impossible convenience to make the plot work?
