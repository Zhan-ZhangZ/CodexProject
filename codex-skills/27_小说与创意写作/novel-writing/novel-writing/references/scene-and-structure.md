# Scene and Structure

Use this file when planning or reviewing the shape of a chapter or scene.

## Core Principle

A chapter is not just a container for material. It is a sequence of functional movements.

## Functional Segment Check

Break the scene into segments. For each segment, answer:

- What is happening?
- What new thing becomes known, felt, or possible?
- What tension rises, bends, or resolves here?

If a segment repeats what the previous one already established, it may need to shrink or disappear.

## Valid Segment Jobs

Common segment jobs:

- anchor the setting and immediate pressure
- introduce a person or force
- test a suspicion
- fail at something
- expose a hidden motive
- shift the power balance
- change what the viewpoint believes

If you cannot describe the job, the segment is probably mushy.

## Progression Rules

Within a chapter, segments should usually create:

- accumulation
- escalation
- reversal
- or clarification

Flat repetition is not progression.

## Transition Checks

When the prose moves between beats, check:

- has time moved?
- has space moved?
- has the viewpoint moved?
- has the scene goal changed?

If one of these shifts without clear marking, readers may feel lost even when all facts are present.

## High-Tension Pacing

When a scene contains a rare opportunity, confrontation, or dangerous contact:

- slow the action enough for the reader to feel the timing
- show preparation, hesitation, concealment, or risk
- do not skip from intention to outcome in one flat jump
- if suspense depends on a concealed motive, do not explain that motive one beat before the decisive action gives it away

Tension scenes need beats, not summary.

## Chapter Ending

By the end of the chapter, something should be different:

- a decision
- a danger
- a relationship
- a misunderstanding
- a clue
- an emotional state that creates forward pull

Do not end on mere stopping. End on changed conditions.

## Common Structural Failures

- too many equal priorities in one chapter
- opening before the reader has an anchor
- middle segments that only restate the setup
- a climax that is narrated faster than the lead-up
- the narration explains a hidden motive too early and drains the reveal of force
- an ending that gives no new pressure

## Review Questions

- What is the chapter trying to do?
- Where does that become clear?
- Which segment is doing no new work?
- Where should the pacing slow down?
- What exact change does the ending leave behind?
